Most common & important LAN topologies

We choose the right topology for your scale, budget, and uptime targets.

Modern networks mix patterns. We design with intent and document the tradeoffs so everyone understands why.

Star

Star topology

All devices connect to a central switch, hub, or router.

  • Reality: The backbone of modern Ethernet LANs.
  • Why: Easy to manage, add/remove nodes, isolate faults.
  • Guardrails: Redundant uplinks and monitored core switches.
Hybrid

Hybrid topology

Combines topologies (e.g., star-bus, star-ring) to meet specific needs.

  • Why: Blend strengths for complex campuses and enterprises.
  • Practice: Pair resilient cores with dedicated rings or spines.
  • Guardrails: Clear segmentation and consistent routing policy.
Tree

Tree (hierarchical)

Root node branching out, combining star and bus in layers.

  • Why: Scales cleanly across departments and sites.
  • Practice: Core/distribution/access with deterministic VLAN plans.
  • Guardrails: Defined oversubscription ratios and QoS at edges.

Other foundational topologies

Know the tradeoffs before you inherit them.

Legacy or specialized patterns appear in mergers and remote sites. We stabilize, modernize, or phase them out with a plan.

Bus

Bus topology

All devices share one main cable.

  • Why: Cost-effective simplicity.
  • Reality: Collision-prone and single point of failure; mostly legacy.
  • Action: Assess and migrate to segmented switched access.
Ring

Ring topology

Devices form a closed loop; data travels one direction (e.g., Token Ring).

  • Why: Deterministic paths in older environments.
  • Reality: Mostly legacy; single break can disrupt the loop.
  • Action: Replace with dual-star or tree with monitored redundancy.
Mesh

Mesh topology

Many interconnections for maximum redundancy.

  • Why: Highest resilience for critical nodes.
  • Reality: Expensive; partial mesh is common to balance cost.
  • Action: Use where uptime is paramount; document failover paths.
